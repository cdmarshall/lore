# Stakeholder Management Playbook

> Ask Claude: "Help me with a stakeholder situation" or "How do I handle [stakeholder] who [problem]?"

## Stakeholder Mapping

### Power/Interest Grid

Map each stakeholder:

```
High Power  │ Keep Satisfied     │ Manage Closely
            │ (Regular updates,  │ (Deep engagement,
            │  address concerns) │  involve in decisions)
────────────┼────────────────────┼──────────────────────
Low Power   │ Monitor            │ Keep Informed
            │ (Minimal effort)   │ (Regular comms,
            │                    │  easy feedback channels)
────────────┴────────────────────┴──────────────────────
              Low Interest         High Interest
```

### Stakeholder Profile Template

For key stakeholders, understand:

| Factor | Notes |
|--------|-------|
| **Goals** | What are they trying to achieve? |
| **Pressures** | What keeps them up at night? |
| **Wins** | What does success look like for them? |
| **Fears** | What are they worried about? |
| **Preferences** | How do they like to communicate? |
| **Influence** | Who do they listen to? |
| **History** | Past experiences with your team/product? |

## Building Relationships

### The Relationship Bank Account

**Deposits:**
- Deliver on commitments
- Give credit publicly
- Share relevant information proactively
- Make their job easier
- Solve problems without being asked
- Listen and show genuine interest

**Withdrawals:**
- Miss commitments
- Surprise them (especially publicly)
- Take credit for shared work
- Create extra work for them
- Go around them
- Dismiss their concerns

### First 90 Days with New Stakeholder

1. **Week 1:** Schedule intro meeting
   - Learn their priorities and concerns
   - Understand their communication preferences
   - Find quick wins to deliver

2. **Month 1:** Establish rhythm
   - Set up regular touchpoint (frequency based on power/interest)
   - Deliver on any early commitments
   - Keep them informed on relevant work

3. **Month 2-3:** Build trust
   - Demonstrate competence and reliability
   - Proactively share information
   - Handle a problem well

## Common Challenges

### The Skeptic

**Signals:** Questions everything, seems resistant, doubts estimates

**Understand:** Often burned before, wants to trust but needs proof

**Approach:**
- Acknowledge past experiences
- Over-communicate early
- Start with small wins
- Be transparent about risks
- Follow through meticulously

### The Micromanager

**Signals:** Wants constant updates, digs into details, second-guesses

**Understand:** Usually anxious about outcomes, needs control to feel safe

**Approach:**
- Increase proactive communication
- Provide more detail than you think necessary
- Give them visibility into your process
- Demonstrate competence through delivery
- Gradually earn autonomy

### The Absent Stakeholder

**Signals:** Misses meetings, slow to respond, disengaged

**Understand:** Might be overwhelmed, not see relevance, or avoiding conflict

**Approach:**
- Make it easy (short meetings, clear asks)
- Connect to their priorities explicitly
- Use async communication if preferred
- Escalate only if decisions are blocked

### The Scope Creeper

**Signals:** Always adding requests, "while you're at it...", expanding requirements

**Understand:** Sees opportunity, may not understand tradeoffs

**Approach:**
- Document scope clearly upfront
- Make tradeoffs visible: "We can add X if we drop Y"
- Don't just say no, offer alternatives
- Involve them in prioritization

### The HiPPO (Highest Paid Person's Opinion)

**Signals:** Opinions become requirements, data doesn't matter

**Understand:** May not realize their influence, used to being right

**Approach:**
- Frame discussions around outcomes/data
- "What would change your mind?"
- Test assumptions with experiments
- Build coalition of aligned stakeholders
- Pick your battles carefully

### The Blocker

**Signals:** Finds reasons things won't work, slows down approvals

**Understand:** Might have legitimate concerns, might be protecting turf

**Approach:**
- Seek to understand their actual concerns
- Address concerns directly
- Find what they need to say yes
- Involve them earlier in the process
- If needed, escalate with specific asks

## Navigating Conflict

### When Stakeholders Disagree with You

1. **Listen fully** - Understand their position completely
2. **Find the valid** - What's right about their concern?
3. **Separate positions from interests** - What do they actually need?
4. **Explore options** - Is there a way to meet both needs?
5. **Escalate cleanly** - If stuck, escalate with clear framing

### When Stakeholders Disagree with Each Other

1. **Understand both positions** - Meet with each separately first
2. **Identify the real conflict** - Resources? Priorities? Values?
3. **Find common ground** - What do they agree on?
4. **Facilitate resolution** - Bring together with clear process
5. **Escalate if needed** - With joint recommendation if possible

### Escalation Framework

**Before escalating:**
- [ ] I've tried to resolve directly
- [ ] I understand all perspectives
- [ ] I have a specific ask
- [ ] I know the impact of not resolving

**When escalating:**
- Frame the issue neutrally
- Present options with tradeoffs
- Make a recommendation
- Be clear about what you need from them

## Communication Strategies

### Status Updates

**For executives:**
- Lead with outcomes and decisions needed
- One level of detail max
- Clear asks

**For peers:**
- More context on approach
- Collaborative problem-solving
- Mutual dependencies

**For teams:**
- Full context on why
- Room for questions
- Credit and recognition

### Saying No Effectively

Instead of "No," try:
- "Yes, and here's what that would require..."
- "Yes, if we deprioritize X..."
- "Not now, but let's revisit when..."
- "Here's what I can do..."

### Delivering Bad News

1. Lead with the news (don't bury it)
2. Take responsibility where appropriate
3. Explain what happened and why
4. Share what you're doing about it
5. Be clear about the ask (if any)
